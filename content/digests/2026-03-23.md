### The Future of Version Control Takes Shape

[Bram Cohen's Manyana project](https://bramcohen.com/p/manyana) introduces a revolutionary approach to version control using Conflict-Free Replicated Data Types (CRDTs). The BitTorrent creator has developed a system where merges never fail by definition—instead, changes that touch each other are flagged as conflicting, providing far more informative conflict resolution. This 470-line Python proof of concept demonstrates vastly improved merge behaviors, including better conflict markers that show exactly what happened and who did it, rather than displaying opaque blobs of code. Perhaps most exciting is Manyana's approach to rebasing, which preserves history rather than creating fictional timelines. This represents the first coherent vision for next-generation version control that could eventually replace Git after its nearly two-decade dominance.

### Apple and Android Ecosystems Bridge the Gap

In a significant move toward cross-platform interoperability, [Samsung is adding native AirDrop support to Galaxy S26 devices](https://www.theverge.com/tech/898815/samsung-quick-share-airdrop-support-galaxy-s26) through an update to its Quick Share feature. Beginning with a rollout in Korea and expanding globally in the coming weeks, this makes Samsung the second Android manufacturer after Google to offer native AirDrop compatibility without Apple's direct involvement. Users will be able to activate the feature in Settings, though both devices need sharing settings set to "Everyone" mode to transfer files. This represents another crack in the walled gardens that have defined mobile ecosystems for years, following Google's development of its own Android interoperability solution for AirDrop that came to Pixel devices in late 2025.

### Elon Musk Announces Ambitious Chip Manufacturing Plans

[Elon Musk has unveiled plans](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/22/elon-musk-unveils-chip-manufacturing-plans-for-spacex-and-tesla/) for a massive chip manufacturing collaboration between Tesla and SpaceX. At an event in Austin, Texas, Musk announced the creation of "Terafab," a semiconductor facility to be built near Tesla's Austin headquarters and gigafactory. The initiative stems from Musk's frustration with semiconductor manufacturers not producing chips quickly enough for his companies' AI and robotics needs. The ambitious goal is to manufacture chips supporting 100 to 200 gigawatts of computing power annually on Earth and a terawatt in space. While Musk provided no timeline for these plans, the announcement represents another vertical integration effort from the tech magnate, albeit one that enters an entirely new industry where he lacks prior expertise.

### Uber's Autonomous Vehicle Strategy Comes Into Focus

[Uber is aggressively expanding its autonomous vehicle partnerships](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/22/techcrunch-mobility-uber-everywhere-all-at-once/), most recently with a significant deal with Rivian. Despite selling its in-house autonomous vehicle unit back in 2020, the ride-hailing giant has been quietly assembling a global network of AV partnerships across delivery, drones, trucking, and robotaxis. The Rivian deal involves an initial $300 million investment and the purchase of 10,000 fully autonomous R2 robotaxis, with an option for 40,000 more, planned for deployment in San Francisco and Miami by 2028. This marks Uber's first partnership where a single company develops both the self-driving system and manufactures the vehicle. The strategic pivot demonstrates Uber's continued commitment to autonomy without shouldering the full technological development burden, spreading its risk across multiple partners and technologies.
